Overview

In the practicum course, you will be doing

  • Propose your project, or select a project from existing options (e.g. Kaggle)
  • Collect data (web-scraping, or download publicly available from data repository, or if available, get data from a private source - e.g. the company you work for)
  • Analyze the data and develop a model/algorithm
  • Visualize the result
  • Showcase your project using git or a blog
  • Present your project as final

Class Format

This course is an online format. There are weekly office hours students drop-in and talk about their progress.

Choosing your project and writing proposal

General advice

  • Start thinking about the project ideas early and iterate
  • Ask for a feedback while proposing project ideas or while working on the project

Things to consider when choosing your project

  • Subject of your interest
    For example, you may be interested in certain industry such as solving health care problem, solving business problem, financial industry, energy industry, science, social media etc.

  • Business/academic value or something fun/cool
    Choosing a project that can appeal also to others (e.g. it has business/academic value or it is cool and fun) is a good idea.

  • Methods and tools of interest
    Choose analysis methods, tools or the types of data that you're interested in or you believe beneficial to learn.

  • Feasibility vs. Ambition While it is a rewarding experience to push your limit, be sure to mind the feasibility. It is a good idea to have a safe backup plan if you're not sure about your first project's feasibility. Also plan on your time well (especially for those who work or can't devote full-time).

  • What do you want to focus?

    • You may focus on learning certain tools or methods/skills required to some job positions you may be interested.
    • If your focus is on developing a new algorithm, then allocate your time more on models/algorithm than data gathering (avoid time-consuming data scraping process if you can) or building a demo web app.
    • If your focus is on a special nature of data such as dealing with big data, you may spend your time on building data pipeline or learning tools dealing with such data.
    • You may focus on building apps or tools that use data science.
    • You may focus on articulating the importance of the problem and interpretation of the result; using a good visualization tool can be useful.

Proposal

Initial proposals serve to organize all of your initial ideas and will allow the instructor to provide feedback on each of them before you narrow it down to one. It is always great to have backups.
Please follow the following template for your project proposals:

  1. Name, Contact info (e.g. email/phone).
  2. Title of the project
  3. High level description of the project: what question or problem are you addressing?
  4. How do you plan to present your work? (visualization or demo, etc.)
  5. How will you analyze the data? What machine learning methods do you plan to use?
  6. Data: Brief description of data. How big do you expect the data will be? Is amount of your data too big or too small? If you're web-scraping or collecting data, how long do you expect to collect the data?
  7. Describe any anticipated difficulties and problems. Discuss how you may overcome the problems.
  8. How long do you expect the project will take? Suggest a brief time line.

Don't worry if you can't answer all of these in week 1. But by the week 2, you should have a clear idea on your project topic.

Research Ethics

When you use a part of other's work such as other's project/code repos, libraries, and publication, you need to cite them. Also explain what is novel- different or improved in your work. Plagiarism is unacceptable. Some examples of plagiarism are:

  • Copying whole or part of someone's algorithm or codes without citing them.
  • Reproducing/copying someone's work without improvement and presenting as your own.

Project Deliverables

At the end of the project, you will deliver

  • your project git repo with organized codes (required)
  • your project summary either in your git repo's readme file or in your blog (required)
  • class presentation (required)
  • other results such as demo, web app (optional)
